Mathematics Group, winter semester 2024-25

The purpose of this mathematics group (which will be organized for the first time in the winter semester of 2024-25), is to get in touch with mathematical subjects that are not taught, or are no longer taught, in our department in NKUA. It is mainly aimed for postgraduate students, but undergraduate students, members of the university or generally anyone else who is interested, are also welcome. The idea is that every week students make two presentations on topics of their interest. Topics may change each month to provide variety (the length of each topic will vary between one (1) and three (3) presentations).

The Marstrand slicing problem

Let \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d\) be a set and \(\mathscr{L}^d\) the \(d-\)dimensional Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbb{R}^d\). The fact that "small sets" \(A\), such as linear spaces of dimension \(δ \leqslant d\), have \(\mathscr{L}^d(A)=0\), while \(\mathscr{L}^δ(Α) \neq 0\), can give rise to a generalised dimension definition. We can say that a set \(A\) has dimension \(δ\) if \(δ \in \mathbb{N}_0\) is the smallest integer such that \(\mathscr{L}^δ(A) \neq 0\), and this will agree with the well-known algebraic definition of the demension, while extending it to other type of sets.

This definition describes the dimension of sets that are not nessesarily linear spaces, however it does not differenciate many sets that should (intuitively) not be "of the same dimension". For example, consider a set of finate points \(A\) and the triadic Cantor set \(C\). While it seems that \(C\) is "dense enough" to contain some complexity (it contains a significant part of the segment \([0,1]\)), one can show that: $$\mathscr{L}^1(A) = \mathscr{L}^1(C) = 0$$ so the Lebesgue measure cannot measure the complexity of sets like the Cantor set. One measure that is practically built for measuring the complexity of sets is the Hausdorff measure.

Definition: Let \(δ \in \mathbb{R}^+\), \(ε \in (0,\infty]\) and \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d\) be a set. We define the \(ε-\)Hausdorff content of \(A\) to be: $$\mathscr{H}^δ_ε(A) = \inf\bigg\{ \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \mathrm{diam}(U_k) ~|~ A \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty} U_k,~ \mathrm{diam}(U_k) < ε \bigg\}$$ If the limit \(\lim_{ε \to 0} \mathscr{H}^δ_ε(A)\) exists, we define the Hausdorff (outer) measure of \(A\): $$\mathscr{H}^δ(A) = \lim_{ε \to 0} \mathscr{H}^δ_ε(A)$$ This outer measure defines a measure, as every outer measure does.

This measure is -in a way- better at calculating dimensions. For example, the triadic Cantor set now has dimension: $$0 < \mathrm{dim}_{\mathscr{H}}(C) = \dfrac{\log 2}{\log 3} < 1$$ which diferentiates it from finate sets. Using this measure and its induced dimension, one can prove the following:

Theorem: (Marstrand slicing problem). Let \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d\), \(d>1\), with \(\dim_{\mathscr{H}}(A) \geqslant 1\). If: $$A_x = \big\{y \in \mathbb{R}^{d-1} ~|~ (x,y) \in A \big\}$$ is the "\(x-\)cut" of \(A\), then: $$\mathrm{dim}_{\mathscr{H}}(A_x) \leqslant \mathrm{dim}_{\mathscr{H}}(A)-1$$ for \(\mathscr{L}-\)almost every \(x\).

Proof: We will show that for every \(1< δ < d\): $$\mathscr{H}^δ(A) \geqslant c_{δ,d} \int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathscr{H}^{δ-1}(A_x)~d\mathscr{L}(x)$$ This will give us a proof, because for \(δ > \mathrm{dim}_{\mathscr{H}}(A)\): $$0=\mathscr{H}^δ(A) \geqslant c_{δ,d} \int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathscr{H}^{δ-1}(A_x)~d\mathscr{L}(x)$$ This means that for \(\mathscr{L}-\)almost every \(x\), \(\mathscr{H}^{δ-1}(Α_x)=0\), thus \(\mathrm{dim}_{\mathscr{H}}(A_x) < δ-1\) for every \(δ > \mathrm{dim}_{\mathscr{H}}(A)\).

So, let \(ε,η > 0\). Because of the definition of the Hausdorff measure and its induced dimension, we can find a cover \((B_k)_{k \in \mathbb{N}}\) of balls, of the set \(A \cap B(0,R),~R>0\), with diameter \(< ε\), such that: $$\sum_{k=1}^\infty \mathrm{diam}^δ(B_k) \leqslant \mathscr{H}_ε^δ\big(Α \cap B(0,R)\big) + η$$ If we now enclose each \(B_k\) in a cube \(Q_k\) of side length \(s_k = \mathrm{diam}(B_k)\) (parallel to the axes), we notice that each \(x-\)cut \((Q_k)_x\) is, in most cases, \(s_k\sqrt{d-1}\) (because this is the diameter of a \((d-1)-\)dimensional cube of side length \(s_k\)). There is also another case, where the \(x-\)cut is empty, and then \(\mathrm{diam}((Q_k)_x)=0\). In any case, we can write: $$\dfrac{1}{(\sqrt{d-1})^{δ-1}}\int_{\mathbb{R}}\sum_{\{x ~|~ (Q_k)_x \neq \emptyset\}} \mathrm{diam}^{δ-1}\big((Q_k)_x\big)~d\mathscr{L}(x) = \sum_{k=1}^{|(B_k)_{k \in \mathbb{N}}|} \big(s_k^{δ-1} \cdot s_k\big) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mathrm{diam}^{δ}(B_k)$$ This, in turn, means that: $$2c_{δ,d} \int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathscr{H}^{δ-1}_ε\big(Α_x \cap B(0,R)\big)~d\mathscr{L}(x) \leqslant \mathscr{H}_ε^δ\big(Α \cap B(0,R)\big)+η$$ where \(2c_{δ,d} = 1/(\sqrt{d-1})^{δ-1}\). Taking \(ε \downarrow 0\), because \(\mathscr{H}_ε^δ(\cdot) \nearrow \mathscr{H}^δ(\cdot)\), by the monotone convergence theorem: $$2c_{δ,d} \int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathscr{H}^{δ-1}\big(Α_x \cap B(0,R)\big)~d\mathscr{L}(x) \leqslant \mathscr{H}^δ\big(Α \cap B(0,R)\big)+η$$ Taking \(η \to 0\), the inequality: $$2c_{δ,d} \int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathscr{H}^{δ-1}\big(Α_x \cap B(0,R)\big)~d\mathscr{L}(x) \leqslant \mathscr{H}^δ\big(Α \cap B(0,R)\big)$$ is shown. Summing in rings, the previous inequality becomes: $$c_{δ,d} \int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathscr{H}^{δ-1}(Α_x)~d\mathscr{L}(x) \leqslant \mathscr{H}^δ(Α)$$ because \(\mathscr{H}^δ\big(A \cap B(0,R+1)\big) \leqslant \mathscr{H}^δ \big(A \cap B(0,R)\big) + \mathscr{H}^δ\Big(A \cap \big(B(0,R+1) \backslash B(0,R)\big)\Big)\). The theorem has now been proven.

Relatively prime numbers and probabilities

In Analytic Number Theory, a marvelous (to me at least) result is proven, about the probability of choosing at random two relatively prime numbers. It is proven that, given a positive integer \(Τ\): $$\lim_{T \to \infty}\dfrac{\#N_T}{T^2}= \dfrac{6}{π^2}$$ where \(N_T = \{(m,n) ~|~ 1 \leqslant m \leqslant T,~ 1 \leqslant n \leqslant T,~ \gcd(m,n)=1\}\).

Lemma 1: Given two arithmetic functions \(f,g\), we define: $$F(x) = \sum_{n \leqslant x} f(n) ~\&~ G(x) = \sum_{n \leqslant x} g(n)$$ Then, for every \(1 \leqslant y \leqslant x\) we have: $$\sum_{n \leqslant x} (f*g)(n) = \sum_{d \leqslant y} g(d)F\left(\dfrac{x}{d}\right)+\sum_{m \leqslant x/y} f(m)G\left(\dfrac{x}{m}\right)-F\left(\dfrac{x}{y}\right)G(y)$$

Proof: It is a well-known method in Analytic Number Theory to represent the sums of Dirichlet products as follows: $$\sum_{n \leqslant x} (f*g)(n) = \sum_{md \leqslant x} f(m)g(d)$$ So for \(y \leqslant x\): \begin{align*} \sum_{md \leqslant x} f(m)g(d) &= \sum_{\begin{matrix} md \leqslant x \\ d \leqslant y \end{matrix}} f(m)g(d) + \sum_{\begin{matrix} md \leqslant x \\ d>y \end{matrix}} f(m)g(d)\\ &= \sum_{d \leqslant y} g(d) \sum_{m \leqslant x/d} f(m) + \sum_{m \leqslant x/y} f(m) \sum_{y < d\leqslant x/m} g(d)\\ &= \sum_{d \leqslant y} g(d) F\left(\dfrac{x}{d}\right)+\sum_{m \leqslant x/y} f(m) \left[ G\left(\dfrac{x}{m}\right)-G(y) \right]\\ &= \sum_{d \leqslant y} g(d) F\left(\dfrac{x}{d}\right) + \sum_{m \leqslant x/y} G\left(\dfrac{x}{m}\right)-G(y)\sum_{m \leqslant x/y} f(m)\\ &= \sum_{d \leqslant y} g(d)F\left(\dfrac{x}{d}\right)+\sum_{m \leqslant x/y} f(m)G\left(\dfrac{x}{m}\right)-F\left(\dfrac{x}{y}\right)G(y) \end{align*} The last is what we wanted to prove, so the Lemma is proven.

Lemma 2: Let \(φ\) be the Euler function. For every \(x>1\): $$\sum_{n \leqslant x} φ(n) = \dfrac{3}{π^2}x^2+O(x^{3/2})$$

Proof: We apply the first Lemma for \(f=N\) and \(g=μ\), because \(φ=μ*Ν\). We get that: $$\sum_{n \leqslant x} φ(n) = \sum_{n \leqslant y} μ(n)F\left(\dfrac{x}{n}\right)+\sum_{m \leqslant x/y} N(m)G\left(\dfrac{x}{m}\right)-F\left(\dfrac{x}{y}\right)G(y)$$ where the symbols \(F,G\) are exactly those from the first Lemma: $$F(x) = \sum_{n \leqslant x} N(n) = \dfrac{x^2}{2}+O(x) ~\&~ G(x) = \sum_{n \leqslant x} μ(n) = O(x)$$ Hence: \begin{align*} \sum_{n \leqslant x} φ(n) = \dfrac{1}{2}\sum_{n \leqslant y} μ(n)\left(\dfrac{x}{n}\right)^2+O\left(\left|\sum_{n \leqslant y} μ(n)\right|x\right)&+O\left(\sum_{m \leqslant x/y} m\dfrac{x}{m}\right)\\ &+O\left(\left( \dfrac{x}{y} \right)^2 y\right)\end{align*} Now, using \(y = \sqrt{x}\), the trivial bounds: $$\left|\sum_{n \leqslant \sqrt{x}} μ(n) \right| \leqslant \sqrt{x} ~\&~ \sum_{m \leqslant \sqrt{x}} m\dfrac{x}{m} \leqslant x^{3/2}$$ give us that: $$\sum_{n \leqslant x} φ(n) = \dfrac{x^2}{2} \sum_{n \leqslant \sqrt{x}} \dfrac{μ(n)}{n^2}+O(x^{3/2})$$ By the known formula: $$ζ(2)\sum_{n=1}^\infty \dfrac{μ(n)}{n^2}=1$$ we also have that: \begin{align*} \sum_{n \leqslant \sqrt{x}} \dfrac{μ(n)}{n^2} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \dfrac{μ(n)}{n^2} - \sum_{n>\sqrt{x}} \dfrac{μ(n)}{n^2} &= \dfrac{6}{π^2}+Ο\left(\sum_{n>\sqrt{x}} \dfrac{1}{n^2}\right)\\ & = \dfrac{6}{π^2}+Ο(x^{-1/2})\end{align*} This concludes the proof of the Lemma, as the result follows from a substitution.

Given the second Lemma, a proof of the limit: $$\lim_{T \to \infty}\dfrac{\#N_T}{T^2}= \dfrac{6}{π^2}$$ can be given. Indeed: $$\sum_{N_T} = 1+2\sum_{\begin{matrix} n < m \leqslant T \\ \gcd(m,n) = 1\end{matrix}} = 1+2\sum_{m \leqslant T} φ(m) = \dfrac{6}{π^2}Τ^2+Ο(Τ^{3/2})$$ which shows the result.
